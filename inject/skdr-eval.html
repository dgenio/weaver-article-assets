skdr-eval estimates how your candidate sklearn policy would have performed on logged decisions — and tells you, with PSIS Pareto-k and ESS, when the logs can't support that answer.

skdr-eval is a small, MIT-licensed Python library that estimates how a candidate scikit-learn policy would have performed on logged (context, action, reward) decisions — using Doubly Robust (DR) and Stabilized DR (SNDR) estimators — so you can read whether a new model is better before paying for an A/B test. Its differentiator is a trust layer: ESS, match rate, PSIS Pareto-k, propensity calibration (ECE), and clip-grid sensitivity roll up into a support_health label (ok / caution / high_risk) and a deploy / ab_test / do_not_deploy verdict you can gate CI on. It's Beta at version 0.11.0, time-series-aware, validated by simulation studies that recover known ground truth within a few standard errors, and works on tabular logs, agent traces, and LLM-reranker logs. You trained a better recommender. Or a smarter router, a new treatment rule, a sharper ad-targeting policy. Offline metrics on a holdout look great. Now comes the uncomfortable part: do you ship it to live traffic?

A/B tests are the honest answer, but they cost real money — lost revenue, SLA violations, patient risk, operator overtime. So you want a cheaper read first: given the decisions we already logged, how would this new policy have performed? That question has a name — off-policy evaluation (OPE) — and a well-known trap. Naive importance weighting will happily hand you a confident-looking number even when your logs contain almost no evidence about what the new policy would do.

skdr-eval is a small, MIT-licensed Python library built around that exact failure mode. It estimates a candidate policy's value from logged (context, action, reward) decisions using Doubly Robust (DR) and Stabilized DR (SNDR) estimators on top of any scikit-learn-compatible model — and just as importantly, it ships a battery of trust diagnostics that tell you when the estimate is not worth acting on.

This is a deep-dive: we'll go through the mechanism, the diagnostics, the deploy verdict logic, and the simulation evidence that the estimators actually recover known ground truth. Everything below is taken from the repo at version 0.11.0.

The problem, concretely

You have a table of logged decisions. Each row is a context (features), the action the old policy took, the reward it got, and a timestamp:

context (x)        action (a)     reward (y)    time
[0.3, 1, 0.8]      operator_3     12.4s         2024-01-01 09:00
[0.9, 0, 0.2]      operator_1      6.1s         2024-01-01 09:05
...

You also have a candidate policy π — a model that, given the same context, would pick actions differently. The thing you want is V(π): the average reward you'd expect if π had been making the decisions instead.

The naive estimator is inverse propensity weighting (IPS): reweight each logged reward by how much more (or less) likely π was to take the logged action versus the old policy. The problem is variance. If π wants to take actions the old policy almost never took, a handful of rows get enormous weights, and your estimate becomes a noisy average of a few lucky samples. Worse, nothing in the raw IPS number warns you this happened.

The idea in one paragraph

Doubly Robust estimation combines two models — an outcome model q̂(x, a) that predicts reward, and a propensity model e(a|x) that predicts what the logging policy did — so the estimate is unbiased if either one is correctly specified. SNDR additionally normalizes the importance weights to cut variance. On top of the point estimate, skdr-eval computes diagnostics that quantify whether the logs actually overlap with what the candidate would do: effective sample size, match rate, PSIS Pareto-k on the importance-weight tail, propensity calibration error, and clip-grid sensitivity. Then it rolls those into a single support_health label — ok, caution, or high_risk — and a deploy / ab_test / do_not_deploy verdict.

How it actually works

The two estimators are exactly the textbook DR / SNDR forms. DR:

V̂_DR = (1/n) Σ [ q̂_π(x_i) + w_i · (y_i − q̂(x_i, a_i)) ]

SNDR self-normalizes the correction term:

V̂_SNDR = (1/n) Σ q̂_π(x_i) + [ Σ w_i·(y_i − q̂(x_i,a_i)) ] / [ Σ w_i ]

where q̂_π(x) is the predicted reward under the candidate policy, q̂(x,a) is the outcome model, and w_i = π(a_i|x_i) / e(a_i|x_i) is the (clipped) importance weight with e a calibrated propensity score.

Two implementation choices matter for real logs:

  • Time-aware everything. Logged decisions are correlated in time, so every cross-validation step uses TimeSeriesSplit with temporal ordering, propensities are calibrated per-fold with isotonic CalibratedClassifierCV, and confidence intervals come from a moving-block bootstrap that preserves short-range dependence. There's even an explicit gap parameter (default 1) that drops adjacent rows between train and test folds to guard against leakage.
  • Clipping is a grid, not a guess. Instead of hard-coding one importance-weight cap, the evaluator sweeps a clip_grid (default (2, 5, 10, 20, 50, ∞)), picks a threshold that keeps effective sample size healthy, and reports how much the estimate moved across the grid as a sensitivity signal. If your number swings wildly depending on where you clip, that's information you want.

From logged decisions to a deploy verdict: skdr-eval estimates a candidate policy's value, then gates it on trust diagnostics before you act.

The whole run returns a single EvaluationArtifact bundling the report DataFrame, per-model results, warnings, sensitivity, propensity diagnostics, and exporters for an HTML report and a machine-readable card.

The part most OPE tutorials skip: the trust diagnostics

This is the reason to reach for skdr-eval over rolling your own DR. The support_health label is computed from thresholds that are grounded in published guidance, not vibes (the defaults live in SupportHealthThresholds):

Diagnostic                          Caution threshold   Escalates to `high_risk`        Source cited in code
---------------------------------   -----------------   -----------------------------   ---------------------
Effective sample size (`ESS / n`)   below 10%           below 5%                        Owen (2013), §9.4
Clip tail mass                      above 5%            above 10%                       Kang & Schafer (2007)
Match rate                          below 50%           below 25%                       —
PSIS Pareto-k                       0.5 ≤ k < 0.7       k ≥ 0.7                         Vehtari et al. (2024)
Propensity ECE                      above 0.10          above 0.20                      Guo et al. (2017)
Min propensity                      —                   at/below `1/n` (zero overlap)   —

Two or more caution-level flags also escalate to high_risk. The Pareto-k diagnostic is the sharpest of the bunch: it fits a Generalized Pareto Distribution to the tail of the raw importance weights, and the shape parameter k directly indicates whether the estimator's variance even exists. At k ≥ 0.7 the variance is undefined and your estimate is being driven by a few decisions — exactly the failure mode the naive IPS number hides.

Those flags then drive a verdict. From the report's recommendation logic: any high_risk blocker yields do_not_deploy with high confidence. If bootstrap CIs are on and the lower bound beats the logged baseline cleanly, you get deploy; if it beats the baseline but caution flags are present, you get ab_test (i.e., the offline read is promising but go validate it live). The verdict is meant to gate a CI pipeline, which is why the CLI returns exit code 3 when any model row's verdict is do_not_deploy.

Try it in 5 minutes

Install from PyPI — the core has no exotic dependencies (numpy, pandas, pyyaml, scikit-learn, scipy, jinja2, pydantic):

pip install skdr-eval

Here's a complete, runnable evaluation adapted from examples/quickstart.py. It generates synthetic logs, evaluates two sklearn models, and reads back the estimate and the trust signals:

import skdr_eval
from sklearn.ensemble import RandomForestRegressor, HistGradientBoostingRegressor

# 1. Logged decisions (here, synthetic service-time logs)
logs, ops_all, _ = skdr_eval.make_synth_logs(n=5000, n_ops=5, seed=42)

# 2. Candidate policies — anything with fit/predict
models = {
    "RandomForest": RandomForestRegressor(n_estimators=100, random_state=42),
    "HistGradientBoosting": HistGradientBoostingRegressor(random_state=42),
}

# 3. Evaluate with DR and SNDR
artifact = skdr_eval.evaluate_sklearn_models(
    logs=logs,
    models=models,
    fit_models=True,
    n_splits=3,
    random_state=42,
    policy_train="pre_split",   # fit the policy on a holdout slice, evaluate on the tail
)

# 4. The estimate ...
print(artifact.report[["model", "estimator", "V_hat", "ESS", "match_rate"]])

# 5. ... and whether you should believe it
print(artifact.warnings)      # per-(model, estimator) support_health + codes
print(artifact.sensitivity)   # clip-grid value range + stability flag
print(artifact.diagnostics)   # propensity overlap / calibration

# 6. Stakeholder artifacts
artifact.export("artifacts/run", formats=["json", "html"])
artifact.save_card("artifacts/run_card.html", "RandomForest")

If your reward column isn't called service_time (the original use case was call-routing), pass y_col="reward" — or y_col="click", y_col="revenue", whatever your logs use.

You don't even need a tabular pipeline. If you have logged agent routing or LLM-reranker decisions as JSONL traces, the trace adapter maps generic (context, action, reward) records into a valid logs frame:

adapted = skdr_eval.adapters.from_jsonl_trace("agent_traces.jsonl", reward_col="cost")
artifact = skdr_eval.evaluate_sklearn_models(
    logs=adapted.logs, models={"candidate": my_cost_model}, y_col=adapted.reward_col,
)
print(artifact.report[["model", "estimator", "V_hat", "support_health"]])

There's also a CLI behind the [cli] extra (skdr-eval doctor logs.parquet, skdr-eval evaluate ... --out ./run) with the stable exit codes mentioned above, so you can wire offline evaluation straight into a deploy gate.

Does it actually recover the truth? The benchmark angle

A point estimator is only worth trusting if it provably recovers a known value on data where you control the answer. skdr-eval treats this as a hard project rule: any change to the statistical core must ship a simulation that recovers a ground-truth parameter. Those simulations live in tests/sim_studies/ and are part of the suite.

The recovery tests construct a synthetic world with an analytic policy value V*, then assert the estimator lands within a few standard errors of it. From test_estimator_recovery_simulation.py, DR, MRDR, and SWITCH-DR each recover V* within 3 standard errors of their influence-function CI; DRos within 4. The MIPS (marginalized IPS) estimator recovers V* within 3 SE when the action embedding is sufficient. The LLM-reranker recipe recovers a known target value within ±2 SE using embedding-MIPS, CPU-only, with no runtime LLM dependency. And the failure-mode demos in examples/known_failures/ go the other way on purpose: a near-argmax logging policy with poor overlap correctly produces support_health = high_risk, HIGH_PARETO_K, and a large Pareto-k rather than a confident lie.

On maturity, be clear-eyed: the package is Beta (Development Status :: 4 - Beta) at version 0.11.0. It is, however, seriously tested — 880 test functions across 55 test files (47 in the main suite plus 8 simulation studies in tests/sim_studies/), plus the simulation studies above, with CI running the quickstart, every notebook, and every use-case script on each PR so the examples can't silently rot.

Honest limitations

OPE is not magic, and skdr-eval is unusually upfront about it:

  • It's contextual-bandit OPE, not RL. One-shot logged decisions only. If your problem has state transitions and long horizons, reach for SCOPE-RL or d3rlpy — the docs say so directly.
  • It needs overlap. If your logs have no support for what the candidate would do, no estimator can fix that. skdr-eval's contribution is that it returns high_risk instead of a confident number.
  • The estimate is an estimate, not a guarantee. It holds under the standard OPE assumptions (unconfoundedness, overlap, a stable data-generating process, useful nuisance models). The diagnostics are trust signals, not proofs of correctness, and offline evaluation does not replace online validation.
  • Slate/top-K and very large action spaces are partial. A slate subpackage and MIPS exist, but full slate estimators and MIPS for huge action spaces are still maturing.

Same problem, two logging regimes. Healthy exploration yields support_health = ok; near-deterministic logging collapses overlap and yields high_risk — no estimator can rescue it.

Takeaways

  • Offline policy evaluation lets you score a candidate policy from logged (context, action, reward) data before you spend an A/B test on it.
  • DR/SNDR are doubly robust — unbiased if either the outcome model or the propensity model is right — and skdr-eval wraps them around any fit/predict sklearn model.
  • The differentiator is the trust layer: ESS, match rate, PSIS Pareto-k, calibration ECE, and clip-grid sensitivity roll up into a support_health label and a deploy / ab_test / do_not_deploy verdict you can gate CI on.
  • The estimators are validated by simulation studies that recover known ground truth within a few standard errors, and the failure-mode demos prove the diagnostics fire when they should.
  • It's Beta at 0.11.0, MIT-licensed, time-series-aware, and works on tabular logs, agent traces, and LLM-reranker logs alike.

If you're shipping anything that makes a decision — a recommender, a router, a targeting rule, an agent's tool selection — skdr-eval is a cheap, honest first read on whether your next version is actually better. It also pairs naturally with agent stacks like agent-kernel, ChainWeaver, and contextweaver, which emit exactly the logged decisions it consumes.

FAQ

What is off-policy evaluation (OPE), and what problem does skdr-eval solve? Off-policy evaluation estimates how a new candidate policy would have performed using decisions already logged by an old policy, so you can avoid spending an A/B test on a model that isn't actually better. skdr-eval is built around OPE's well-known trap: naive importance weighting hands you a confident-looking number even when the logs contain almost no evidence about what the new policy would do.

What estimators does skdr-eval use, and why are they "doubly robust"? It uses Doubly Robust (DR) and Stabilized DR (SNDR) estimators on top of any scikit-learn-compatible model. They combine an outcome model q̂(x, a) that predicts reward with a propensity model e(a|x) that predicts what the logging policy did, so the estimate is unbiased if either one is correctly specified; SNDR additionally normalizes the importance weights to cut variance.

What are the trust diagnostics and the deploy verdict? skdr-eval computes effective sample size (ESS), match rate, PSIS Pareto-k on the importance-weight tail, propensity calibration error (ECE), and clip-grid sensitivity, with thresholds grounded in published guidance (Owen 2013, Vehtari et al. 2024, and others). These roll into a support_health label (ok / caution / high_risk) and a verdict: a high_risk blocker yields do_not_deploy; beating the baseline cleanly yields deploy; beating it with caution flags yields ab_test.

Why does PSIS Pareto-k matter? What are skdr-eval's limitations and maturity?
