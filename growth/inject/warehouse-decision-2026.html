How the three pricing models actually work, which workloads each one wins, and a decision table plus flowchart to pick without regret.

Short answer: there is no universal winner — the right platform is the one whose pricing model matches your workload shape. Choose BigQuery for spiky, ad-hoc, or BI-style SQL where you want zero infrastructure and pay only for the bytes a query scans. Choose Snowflake for steady, SQL-first ELT and concurrent BI where per-second warehouse billing and simple operations matter most. Choose Databricks when machine learning, data science, or an open-format lakehouse on raw files is the center of gravity. The expensive mistakes in 2026 almost always come from running a workload on the platform whose billing meter punishes that exact pattern.

This guide is a decision tool, not a leaderboard. I'm going to explain the three pricing models (not invented benchmark numbers), map them to workload types, cover governance, and give you a decision table and a flowchart you can actually use. Rates and product names change constantly — every dollar figure below is an illustrative US-baseline taken from public pricing pages, so always confirm current pricing for your region and tier before committing budget.

How do the three pricing models actually work?

Every one of these platforms separates storage from compute and bills them on different meters. Storage is the boring part: all three charge roughly per compressed terabyte per month, with cheaper tiers for cold data. The interesting — and budget-defining — difference is how compute is metered.

The three pricing models side by side: what you actually pay for in Snowflake, BigQuery, and Databricks.

Snowflake — credits per warehouse-second. You spin up a virtual warehouse (a named compute cluster) sized X-Small through 6X-Large. Each size up doubles the credit-per-hour rate. You're billed per second while the warehouse is running, with a 60-second minimum each time it resumes (Snowflake docs via CloudZero). Credits convert to dollars by edition and region — roughly $2/credit for Standard on AWS US East as a baseline, more for Enterprise and Business Critical. The number you must internalize: you pay for uptime, not for queries. An idle warehouse left running bills you for nothing useful, which is why idle auto-suspend is the single most important Snowflake cost setting.

BigQuery — bytes scanned, or slot-hours. BigQuery has two modes. On-demand charges per data scanned by a query — about $6.25 per TiB at the time of writing, with the first 1 TiB/month free and a 10 MB minimum per query (Google Cloud pricing). There is no cluster to size or suspend; you pay purely for what each query reads. The flip side: a careless SELECT * over a wide, unpartitioned table can scan terabytes and generate a startling bill — a documented failure mode the cost-optimization literature returns to repeatedly (LeanOps). The alternative is BigQuery Editions (Standard / Enterprise / Enterprise Plus), which bill slot-hours of compute capacity with autoscaling and commitment discounts — a model closer to Snowflake's, for predictable heavy workloads.

Databricks — DBUs, plus the VM underneath. Databricks meters a Databricks Unit (DBU), a unit of processing consumed per second, multiplied by a rate that varies by compute type and tier. SQL Classic, SQL Pro, and Serverless SQL each carry different per-DBU rates (Flexera). Two wrinkles trip people up. First, on classic (non-serverless) compute, you also pay your cloud provider for the underlying VMs — the DBU charge sits on top. Second, Photon (the vectorized C++ engine, always on for serverless and SQL warehouses) changes how many DBUs a query consumes, not just the rate — for analytical SQL it usually pays for itself by finishing faster.

When should you choose each one?

Choose BigQuery when usage is spiky or you live on GCP. The on-demand, bytes-scanned model is genuinely serverless — nothing to provision, nothing to suspend, no cluster to forget about over a weekend. For ad-hoc analytics, dashboards with bursty traffic, and teams that don't want to operate infrastructure, it's the lowest-friction option. The discipline tax is query hygiene: partition and cluster your tables, never SELECT * on wide tables, and preview byte estimates. If your scanned volume becomes large and predictable, switch those workloads to Editions (slot-based) so you stop paying retail per-byte.

Choose Snowflake when you're SQL-first with steady, concurrent workloads. Snowflake's strength is operational simplicity for analytics teams: separate warehouses per team or workload, each auto-suspending when idle, with near-frictionless concurrency scaling. It pairs naturally with dbt-style ELT. Per-second billing rewards short, busy bursts of activity; it punishes warehouses left running idle, so your cost model lives or dies by auto-suspend settings and right-sizing. If your team's mental model is "tables and SQL," Snowflake has the gentlest learning curve.

Choose Databricks when ML or the lakehouse is the point. If your data scientists live in notebooks, you're training models, or you want compute engines reading open files (Delta/Iceberg) in your own object storage rather than data locked in a proprietary store, Databricks is built for that from the ground up. Databricks SQL with Photon has closed much of the BI/SQL gap, so a Spark-centric shop no longer needs a second warehouse just for dashboards. The cost trap is operational: classic clusters carry VM overhead and idle expense, so lean on serverless (autoscale-to-zero) and cluster policies.

A useful gut check: what is the center of gravity of your team? SQL analysts and BI → Snowflake or BigQuery. Bytes-scanned-friendly, GCP-native, serverless → BigQuery. ML, Python, notebooks, open files → Databricks. The platform that fits your people is usually cheaper in total cost of ownership than the one that's theoretically cheaper per query.

What about governance and open table formats?

This is where 2026 actually differs from 2022, and it's worth a paragraph before you lock in.

The industry has converged on open table formats — primarily Apache Iceberg — and on the Iceberg REST Catalog as the way to decouple governance from compute. That matters because it changes the lock-in conversation: in principle, multiple engines can read and write the same files governed by one catalog.

The two heavyweight governance layers take different stances (Snowflake engineering blog). Snowflake Horizon implements the open Iceberg REST Catalog and Apache Polaris APIs natively, explicitly designed to govern tables that external engines (Spark, Flink, BigQuery) also touch. Databricks Unity Catalog offers deep, fine-grained governance, lineage, and discovery — but the managed offering is centered on Databricks compute, and fine-grained policies don't automatically carry across when an outside engine crosses the boundary (Onehouse). If a true multi-engine, open-catalog architecture is a hard requirement, evaluate this before the pricing math — it can override it.

How do you keep the bill from surprising you?

The platform choice sets your meter; your engineering discipline sets your bill. The levers, ranked by impact:

  1. Match the meter to the workload. Don't run an always-on streaming-style job on a bytes-scanned meter, and don't run thousands of tiny spiky queries on an always-warm cluster you forgot to suspend.
  2. Kill idle compute. Snowflake auto-suspend (short timeouts), Databricks serverless autoscale-to-zero, BigQuery's inherent no-idle on-demand model. Idle time is the most common line item people are shocked by.
  3. Control scan volume. Partition and cluster BigQuery tables; prune columns everywhere; materialize expensive repeated aggregations.
  4. Right-size, then commit. Start small, measure, scale up only when a workload proves it needs it. Once usage is predictable, buy commitments (Snowflake capacity, BigQuery Editions/commitments, Databricks committed-use) for meaningful discounts.
  5. Attribute cost to teams. Per-warehouse / per-project / per-workspace tagging turns an opaque bill into a feedback loop the people generating the cost can actually act on.

If you want platform-specific tactics, I've written deep dives on cutting a BigQuery bill and the BigQuery anti-patterns that quietly inflate scan costs. And if your data is small enough that you're over-buying a warehouse at all, it's worth reading DuckDB vs Spark before you provision anything — not every problem needs a distributed warehouse.

The honest trade-offs (when NOT to use each)

  • Don't default to BigQuery if your workload is large, steady, and full-scan-heavy on-demand — you'll overpay per byte until you move to Editions, and you're committing to GCP.
  • Don't default to Snowflake if your team is ML/Python-first or you want compute-agnostic open files — you'll be fighting the grain of the platform and paying for warehouses you barely use.
  • Don't default to Databricks for a small, pure-SQL BI shop with no data science — you'll carry operational complexity (and possibly classic-cluster VM cost) you don't need.
  • Don't choose any of them if your data fits comfortably on one machine — a local engine may be dramatically cheaper and simpler.

FAQ

Which is cheapest: Snowflake, BigQuery, or Databricks? None is universally cheapest — they bill on different meters (warehouse-seconds, bytes scanned, DBUs), so the cheapest one is whichever meter matches your workload. Spiky ad-hoc SQL is usually cheapest on BigQuery on-demand; steady concurrent SQL on Snowflake; ML and lakehouse on Databricks. Total cost is dominated by idle compute and scan volume, not headline rates.

Do all three separate storage from compute? Yes. All three bill storage and compute on independent meters and charge storage roughly per compressed TB per month with cheaper cold tiers. The differences are entirely in how compute is metered: Snowflake by credit-seconds, BigQuery by bytes scanned or slot-hours, Databricks by DBUs (plus the underlying VM on classic compute).

Is BigQuery on-demand or Editions better? On-demand (per TiB scanned) is best for spiky, unpredictable, or low-volume usage because you pay only for what each query reads and there's nothing to manage. Editions (slot-based, with autoscaling and commitments) wins once your scanned volume is large and predictable, giving you capacity-based pricing and discounts instead of retail per-byte charges.

Does Photon make Databricks cheaper? Often, yes — for analytical SQL. Photon carries a higher per-DBU rate but typically reduces total DBU consumption by running queries faster, so it frequently nets out cheaper for SQL-heavy work. It's enabled by default on serverless and SQL warehouses. Always validate on your own workloads rather than assuming.

Should governance or pricing drive the decision? If you have a hard requirement for a multi-engine, open-catalog architecture (one Iceberg catalog governing files that Spark, BigQuery, and others all touch), let governance drive — compare Snowflake Horizon's open REST-catalog approach against Databricks Unity Catalog first, because it can constrain the field before pricing ever enters the conversation. For single-platform shops, let workload-fit and pricing lead.
